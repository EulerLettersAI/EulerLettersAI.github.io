About

Who we are

Euler Letters AI is led by Samer El Kababji, Assistant Professor at Princess Sumaya University for Technology (PSUT). The organization hosts multidisciplinary repositories for research projects and, occasionally, selected MSc and capstone work—especially projects that benefit from open methods, reproducible pipelines, and clear documentation.

We keep sensitive datasets out of public repositories and publish responsibly (especially in medical contexts). We also maintain practical project notes, so each repository tells a full story from data assumptions to model limits.

  • Clarity — readable code, structured documentation, reproducible experiments.
  • Interdisciplinarity — AI as a bridge between fields, not a silo.
  • Open scholarship — tools and insights others can learn from and build on.
  • Practical impact — methods that survive real constraints.

Why “Euler Letters AI”

Inspiration and continuity

The name is inspired by Leonhard Euler’s Letters to a German Princess—a collection where he explains ideas across mathematics, physics, astronomy, and philosophy in a disciplined, teachable way.

What stood out to us is how Euler links concepts across domains without losing rigor. That habit—connecting dots while respecting evidence—is exactly what modern AI teams need.

“Euler Letters AI” also reflects a broader idea: the advancement of knowledge is a long conversation across generations and civilizations. We build with that continuity in mind.

One example of this continuity appears in Euler’s reflections on human will. In his Letters, Euler distinguishes between inclination, the act of willing, and execution. He argues that while external forces may influence us through motives, the act of willing itself cannot be directly constrained by any exterior power—even divine power—because liberty belongs to the interior operation of the rational agent.

Centuries earlier, theologians of the Muʿtazilite school—such as ʿAbd al-Jabbār (d. 1025) in works like al-Mughnī fī Abwāb al-Tawḥīd wa-l-ʿAdl—articulated a structurally similar idea: moral responsibility requires that human acts originate from the agent’s own power and choice rather than coercion. External causes may provide motives or circumstances, but accountability presupposes genuine volition.

Whether or not there is a direct historical connection, the conceptual proximity is striking. Across centuries and cultures, thinkers grappled with the same structural problem: how to reconcile causality, rationality, and responsibility. This is the kind of continuity we recognize an ongoing, civilizational effort to understand agency, knowledge, and order in the world.

Lab objective

Methods

Develop reproducible AI pipelines: data engineering, model development, evaluation, interpretability, and deployment-minded experimentation.

Applications

Apply AI across engineering and medical contexts, including time series, signals, sensor systems, predictive modeling, and decision support with domain constraints in mind.

Open scholarship

Publish clean repositories, tutorials, and teaching-grade examples that help students and researchers learn by building.

Euler as a teacher across disciplines

“I shall endeavor to explain everything with the greatest possible clarity…” — Euler, Letters to a German Princess
“We must not confuse what we imagine with what is demonstrated.” — Euler, Letters to a German Princess

Today, AI projects often sit at the intersection of algorithms, statistics, data engineering, ethics, and the application field itself. We adopt a letters mindset: explain assumptions, define terms, and document reasoning so outcomes are reusable—not just impressive demos.
